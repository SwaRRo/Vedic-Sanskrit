Kāṇḍa 2: Nāda-Pravāha (नाद-प्रवाह)

Mastering the Flow of Sound

Lesson 2.2: The Breath's Echo – Visarga Sandhi (विसर्ग सन्धि)

Welcome to the elegant world of Visarga Sandhi. The visarga (ः, `ḥ`), a puff of air from the glottis, is like a phonetic echo of the vowel that precedes it. Being just breath, it is highly susceptible to the influence of the sound that follows. It assimilates, taking on the characteristics of its new neighbor, or changes into something else entirely.

We will cover the most common and important transformations of the visarga.

1. Transformation into Sibilants (श, ष, स)

When a visarga is followed by an unvoiced (aghoṣa) consonant, it often transforms into the sibilant (`ś`, `ṣ`, or `s`) that shares the same point of articulation (uccāraṇasthāna) as that consonant.

visarga (ḥ) + च/छ (ca/cha) → श् (ś) + च/छ

  • Example: पुनः + च (punaḥ + ca) → पुनश्च (punaśca, "and again")
Logic: `ca` is palatal, so the breathy visarga becomes the palatal sibilant `ś`.

visarga (ḥ) + ट/ठ (ṭa/ṭha) → ष् (ṣ) + ट/ठ

  • Example: धनुः + टङ्कारः (dhanuḥ + ṭaṅkāraḥ) → धनुष्टङ्कारः (dhanuṣṭaṅkāraḥ, "the twang of the bow")
Logic: `ṭa` is retroflex, so the visarga becomes the retroflex sibilant `ṣ`.

visarga (ḥ) + त/थ (ta/tha) → स् (s) + त/थ

  • Example: नमः + ते (namaḥ + te) → नमस्ते (namaste, "greetings to you")
Logic: `ta` is dental, so the visarga becomes the dental sibilant `s`.

Note: Before `ka/kha` or `pa/pha`, the visarga often remains unchanged (or becomes a special symbol). The changes to sibilants are most consistent before `c/ch`, `ṭ/ṭh`, and `t/th`.

2. The "O" Rule (Utva Sandhi)

This is one of the most common sandhis in the entire language. When the visarga is preceded by a short अ (`a`) and followed by a voiced (ghoṣa) consonant OR another short अ (`a`), the `aḥ` combination transforms into ओ (`o`).

अः (aḥ) + Ghoṣa Consonant → ओ (o)

  • Example: मनः + रथः (manaḥ + rathaḥ) → मनोरथः (manorathaḥ, "chariot of the mind, desire")
Logic: `r` is a voiced consonant. `manaḥ` ends in `aḥ`. So `aḥ` becomes `o`.
  • Example: शिवः + हम् (śivaḥ + ham) → शिवोऽहम् (śivo'ham, "I am Shiva") - See rule 2b for the next step.

अः (aḥ) + अ (a) → ओऽ (o')

If the following sound is another short 'a', the `aḥ` becomes `o` as before, and the following 'a' is "elided" (disappears) and is marked by an avagraha symbol (ऽ, `S`).

  • Example: कः + अपि (kaḥ + api) → कोऽपि (ko'pi, "someone, anyone")

3. Transformation into 'R' (Rutva Sandhi)

When the visarga is preceded by any vowel EXCEPT short or long अ/आ (i.e., preceded by `i, ī, u, ū, e, ai`, etc.) and is followed by any voiced sound (vowel or voiced consonant), the visarga becomes र (`r`).

(i, u, e, etc.)ḥ + Voiced Sound → ...r...

  • Example: कविः + जयति (kaviḥ + jayati) → कविर्जयति (kavirjayati, "the poet is victorious")
  • Example: मुनिः + अयम् (muniḥ + ayam) → मुनिरयम् (munirayam, "this sage")
  • Example: धेनुः + गच्छति (dhenuḥ + gacchati) → धेनुर्गच्छति (dhenurgacchati, "the cow goes")

4. Elision (Disappearance) of Visarga

Sometimes, the visarga simply disappears. The two most common situations are:

अः (aḥ) + any vowel (EXCEPT short 'a') → a ...

When `aḥ` is followed by a vowel that isn't `a`, the visarga simply drops, and no further sandhi occurs.

  • Example: अर्जुनः + उवाच (arjunaḥ + uvāca) → अर्जुन उवाच (arjuna uvāca, "Arjuna said")

आः (āḥ) + any voiced sound → ā ...

When `āḥ` is followed by any voiced sound (vowel or voiced consonant), the visarga usually just drops.

  • Example: देवाः + गच्छन्ति (devāḥ + gacchanti) → देवा गच्छन्ति (devā gacchanti, "the gods go")

Deeper Concept: Visarga as a "Gateway" Sound

The visarga acts as a phonetic gateway. It holds the place of a final sound but remains undefined until it knows what is coming next. It's a placeholder for an impending relationship. It shows that in Sanskrit, a word's sound is never in true isolation; its final breath is always in anticipation of the first breath of the next word, weaving speech into an unbroken acoustic tapestry. It perfectly embodies the Vedantic idea that nothing exists in isolation; everything is defined by its relationship to everything else.
