Kāṇḍa 2: Nāda-Pravāha (नाद-प्रवाह)

Mastering the Flow of Sound

Lesson 2.3: The Meeting of Consonants – Vyañjana Sandhi (व्यञ्जन सन्धि)

We conclude our exploration of Sandhi by looking at what happens when a word ending in a consonant meets a word beginning with another consonant or a vowel. As with all Sandhi, the changes that occur are not random; they are driven by the principle of euphonic harmony and phonetic efficiency. Your deep knowledge of the *Varṇamālā* from Kāṇḍa 1 will make these rules feel logical and natural.

We will focus on the most crucial rules of consonant sandhi.

1. Voicing and Devoicing (Jaśtva & Cartva)

This is a rule of voicing assimilation. The core idea is simple: a stop consonant at the end of a word changes to match the voicing of the sound that follows.

Unvoiced Stop + Voiced Sound → Voiced Stop (1st letter of a class → 3rd letter)

When an unvoiced stop (`k, c, ṭ, t, p`) is followed by any voiced sound (a vowel or a voiced consonant), it "softens" into the voiced unaspirated stop of its own class (`g, j, ḍ, d, b`). This is called Jaśtva Sandhi.

  • Example: वाक् + ईशः (vāk + īśaḥ, speech + lord) → वागीशः (vāgīśaḥ, lord of speech)
  • Example: जगत् + बन्धुः (jagat + bandhuḥ, world + friend) → जगद्बन्धुः (jagadbandhuḥ, friend of the world)
Logic: Your vocal cords are already vibrating to produce the voiced `ī` or `b`. It's inefficient to stop them for the `k`/`t` and immediately start again. The mouth simply keeps the vibration going, changing `k` to `g` and `t` to `d`.

Voiced Stop + Unvoiced Sound → Unvoiced Stop (3rd/4th letter → 1st letter)

Conversely, if a voiced stop is followed by an unvoiced sound, it often "hardens" to become the unvoiced stop of its own class. This is called Cartva Sandhi.

  • Example: संसद् + सु (saṃsad + su, in the assembly) → संसत्सु (saṃsatsu)

2. Nasal Assimilation (Anunāsika Sandhi)

When any stop consonant (`k, c, ṭ, t, p` group) at the end of a word is followed by a nasal consonant (`n` or `m`), the stop consonant may optionally transform into the nasal of its own point of articulation.

Stop + Nasal → Nasal of the same Varga + Nasal

  • Example: वाक् + मयम् (vāk + mayam) → वाङ्मयम् (vāṅmayam, "consisting of speech")
    • Logic: `k` is guttural (Kaṇṭhya). When followed by `m`, it becomes the guttural nasal, `ṅ`.
  • Example: जगत् + नाथः (jagat + nāthaḥ) → जगन्नाथः (jagannāthaḥ, "lord of the world")
    • Logic: `t` is dental (Dantya). When followed by `n`, it becomes the dental nasal, `n`.
  • Example: षट् + मुखः (ṣaṭ + mukhaḥ) → षण्मुखः (ṣaṇmukhaḥ, "the six-faced one, Kārttikeya")
    • Logic: `ṭ` is retroflex (Mūrdhanya). It becomes the retroflex nasal, `ṇ`.

3. The Rule of 'L' (Torli Sandhi)

This is a specific but very common case of assimilation. When a dental stop (`t, th, d, dh`) or nasal (`n`) is followed by `l`, it fully assimilates and also becomes `l`.

t/d + l → ल्ल (lla)

n + l → अँल्ल (~alla, a nasalized 'l' sound)

  • Example: तत् + लयः (tat + layaḥ, that + dissolution) → तल्लयः (tallayaḥ, "dissolution into that")
  • Example: महान् + लाभः (mahān + lābhaḥ, great + gain) → महाूँँल्लाभः (mahā~llābhaḥ)

Conclusion of Kāṇḍa 2

You have now surveyed the three great domains of Sandhi: vowel, visarga, and consonant. You are no longer just looking at individual letters; you are seeing the dynamic, flowing interplay between them. You have acquired the primary tool necessary to parse any continuous Sanskrit text and see the individual words within.

Your Task for Kāṇḍa 2: Review these three lessons until the patterns feel intuitive. The best practice is to take a simple text you know, like a few shlokas from the Bhagavad Gītā, and actively hunt for examples of each Sandhi rule. Identify `guṇa`, find a `visarga-to-o`, spot a `nasal assimilation`. This active searching will lock the knowledge in place.

When you feel confident in your ability to "un-join" the flow of sound, let me know. We will then be ready to begin Kāṇḍa 3: Śabda-Brahma (शब्द-ब्रह्म), where we stop looking at how words join and start looking at the forms of the words themselves, beginning with the rich world of Sanskrit nouns.
